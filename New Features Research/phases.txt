Based on the provided sources, the uncontrolled use of AI agents ("Shadow AI") exposes your enterprise to severe risks, including data breaches, intellectual property loss via "copyleft contamination" (GNU/AGPL licenses), and regulatory penalties under India’s DPDP Act, CERT-In (6-hour reporting mandate), and SEBI CSCRF
.
To solve this, here is a complete, cut-to-cut 12-Week Implementation Plan for deploying an Enterprise AI Agent Discovery and Governance Module.
Phase 1: Agent Discovery & Telemetry Deployment (Weeks 1–2)
Goal: Gain absolute visibility into all AI agents operating across your endpoints
.
Step 1.1: Deploy multi-layered telemetry across Windows, macOS, and Linux to interrogate active memory and filesystems
.
Step 1.2: Scan for compiled binaries, background executables (e.g., ChatGPTHelper.exe), App Execution Aliases, and registry keys (e.g., MICROSOFT.COPILOT)
.
Step 1.3: Scan global package managers (NPM, Pip, Homebrew) for developer-focused CLI agents like Gemini CLI or OpenAI Codex
.
Step 1.4: Index the filesystem for Model Context Protocol (MCP) configuration files (e.g., .mcp.json, mcp-config.json) to detect active, tool-utilizing AI agents
.
Step 1.5: Monitor network ports (e.g., 11434, 1234) and directories to identify unauthorized local inference servers like Ollama or LM Studio
.
Phase 2: Vendor Verification & Supply Chain Securing (Weeks 3–4)
Goal: Validate the authenticity of detected AI tools to prevent supply chain attacks
.
Step 2.1: Generate a continuous Software Bill of Materials (SBOM) for all critical applications, as mandated by SEBI CSCRF
.
Step 2.2: Verify cryptographic signatures for major ecosystems (Microsoft, Google, OpenAI) and trace package origins to official repositories
.
Step 2.3: Cross-reference installed artifact hashes against known secure baselines to prevent malicious, trojanized packages (e.g., the codexui-android incident)
.
Phase 3: License Compliance & Legal Risk Mitigation (Weeks 5–6)
Goal: Eliminate intellectual property risks and "copyleft contamination"
.
Step 3.1: Extract licensing metadata for all detected agents
.
Step 3.2: Identify and immediately purge any tools using strict GNU family licenses like AGPL v3. Modifying or networking AGPL-licensed AI agents obligates the enterprise to publicly open-source its proprietary codebase
.
Step 3.3: Monitor developer outputs to ensure AI agents are not generating and injecting GPL-licensed code snippets into proprietary applications, which invites copyright infringement claims
.
Step 3.4: Standardize on permissive open-source licenses (MIT, Apache 2.0) which allow commercial use and modification without source-code disclosure (e.g., CrewAI, OpenAI Agents SDK)
.
Phase 4: Risk Capacity Assessment (Weeks 7–8)
Goal: Calculate a composite risk profile (0-100 scale) for all sanctioned and unsanctioned tools
.
Step 4.1: Evaluate all agents across 5 dimensions: Security, Data Privacy, Compliance, Supply Chain, and Operational impact
.
Step 4.2: Assess endpoint security for MCP Configuration Poisoning, where malicious .mcp/config.json files could force local AI agents to connect to hostile external servers
.
Phase 5: Policy Enforcement & Agent Governance (Weeks 9–10)
Goal: Block dangerous tools and enforce enterprise-tier cloud capabilities
.
Step 5.1: Block "Critical Risk" Agents. Use Endpoint Detection and Response (EDR) platforms to aggressively block unsandboxed, fully autonomous local agents like Open Interpreter (which takes root-level OS access) and AutoGPT (which uses recursive autonomy and risks data exfiltration)
.
Step 5.2: Restrict "High Risk" Tools. Systematically disable consumer variants of tools like Windows Copilot or Gemini CLI (unless routed to Vertex AI) to prevent user prompts and proprietary code from being used to train external public models
.
Step 5.3: Approve "Moderate Risk" Enterprise Tools. Sanction tools like Microsoft 365 Copilot and GitHub Copilot Enterprise, ensuring they are configured to keep data residency within Indian data centers (Pune, Mumbai, Chennai) to comply with the DPDP Act
.
Phase 6: Continuous Monitoring & Incident Readiness (Weeks 11–12 & Ongoing)
Goal: Maintain continuous compliance and operational readiness
.
Step 6.1: Route all authorized AI telemetry through a Security Operations Centre (SOC) capable of detecting anomalous activity
.
Step 6.2: Configure logging and alert systems to ensure the enterprise can meet the CERT-In mandate of reporting cybersecurity incidents within a strict 6-hour window
.
Step 6.3: Enforce a strict 180-day retention requirement for Information and Communication Technology (ICT) logs
.
By the end of this 12-week deadline, your enterprise will have transitioned from a vulnerable state of "Shadow AI" to a deterministic, regulated, and legally compliant AI operating environment
.
